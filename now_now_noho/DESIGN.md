# DESIGN.md — Now Now NoHo
---

# 1. Brand Design Thesis

Now Now NoHo is a contemporary boutique-hostel experience expressed through a clear late-1980s and early-1990s downtown New York design undertone.

The visual language combines:

- Bold, condensed typography
- Saturated color fields
- Modular graphics
- Airbrushed and gradient treatments
- Slightly surreal imagery
- Fashion-editorial composition
- Downtown zine and club-flyer energy
- A playful, anti-corporate point of view
- Contemporary hospitality usability

The result should feel like:

> Late-’80s and early-’90s downtown New York postmodernism, rebuilt for a modern solo-travel experience.

The design is not nostalgic for nostalgia’s sake. It uses the visual confidence, experimentation, and cultural energy of that era to make a compact hospitality product feel expressive, independent, and full of possibility.

# 5. Native Product Shape

The product’s natural structure is a journey, not a traditional room catalog.

## Primary Journey

1. Discover the city
2. Book a private sleeper cabin
3. Prepare for the stay
4. Enter the shared environment
5. Explore New York
6. Return to rest
7. Connect with others
8. Carry the story forward

## Core Product Tension

> Private space. Shared rhythm.

The cabin is private and lockable, but the broader environment is shared.

The product combines:

- Private sleeper cabins
- Open ceilings for centralized airflow
- Shared acoustics
- Shared bathrooms
- Shared common spaces
- Quiet-floor expectations
- Individual rest rituals
- Collective guest behavior

## Information Hierarchy

Before decorative storytelling, every room-related page must clearly establish:

1. This is a private sleeper cabin.
2. It is designed for one guest unless explicitly sold as two separate cabins.
3. Bathrooms are shared.
4. Walls do not fully extend to the ceiling.
5. Sound and light may travel.
6. Cabins are compact.
7. There is no elevator.
8. Temperature is centrally managed.
9. Calls, eating, and media are restricted in cabin areas.
10. Large luggage may require external storage and fees may apply.

Expectation-setting is part of the brand experience, not legal fine print.

1. Campaign or page idea
2. Product truth

## Color Behavior

- Use large color fields rather than small decorative accents.
- Pair one dominant saturated color with one quieter supporting tone.
- Use cream instead of pure white when warmth is needed.
- Use burgundy and dark green for grounded, immersive sections.
- Use pink, acid green, coral, and gradients for disruption and surprise.
- Avoid generic hospitality palettes built around navy, beige, and gold.
- Avoid excessive simultaneous color competition.

## Gradient Behavior

Gradients should feel:

- Airbrushed
- Dreamlike
- Analog
- Soft-edged
- Slightly imperfect
- Like light leaks, old print effects, or early digital graphics

Avoid:

- Glossy SaaS gradients
- Neon cyberpunk effects
- Overly smooth corporate mesh gradients
- Gradient text used without purpose

# 8. Typography

The brand favors tall, condensed, assertive display typography paired with simpler supporting text.



## Display Type Behavior

- Condensed
- Uppercase or title case
- High visual impact
- Tight horizontal footprint
- Strong vertical rhythm
- Often used at large scale
- Suitable for poster-like composition

## Body Type Behavior

- Clear and modern
- More restrained than display type
- Comfortable at small sizes
- Used to balance expressive graphics
- Short paragraphs preferred

## Type Hierarchy

### Display / Hero

	ginestra-black
	ginestra-black
	ginestra-bold
	ginestra-bold

- Large enough to become part of the image
- Short phrases
- Strong line breaks
- Avoid long sentence headlines

### Section Headings

- Condensed
- High contrast from body copy
- Often paired with a small contextual label

### Eyebrows

- 1–5 words
- Lowercase when used in current CMS style
- Functional, not decorative
- Examples: `our rooms`, `sleep better`, `downtown guide`

### Body Copy

 saans-regular
 saans-medium

- Concise
- Rhythmic
- Plainspoken
- No inflated luxury language
- No generic travel clichés

### Utility Copy

- Highly legible
- Direct
- Explicit
- Used for room facts, restrictions, booking terms, and policy details

# 9. Spacing and Layout

## Layout Character

- Editorial, not dashboard-like
- Strong asymmetry
- Split-screen compositions
- Full-bleed imagery
- Large fields of color
- Modular blocks
- Intentional negative space
- Occasional dense collage moments
- Clear contrast between quiet and energetic sections

## Spacing Behavior

Without verified code tokens, use relative rules:

- Hero spacing should feel generous and cinematic.
- Editorial copy widths should remain narrow enough to read comfortably.
- Product facts should be grouped tightly for scanning.
- Visual sections may break the grid.
- Utility sections should return to a clear, stable grid.
- Do not use identical padding for every section.
- Alternate compressed and expansive sections to create rhythm.

## Image Ratios

Use the subject to determine the ratio:

- Portrait for traveler and fashion imagery
- Square for modular editorial cards
- Landscape for interiors and neighborhood scenes
- Full-viewport or near-full-viewport for portal moments
- Tight crop for amenities and tactile details

Avoid forcing every visual into one card ratio.

- Concise
- Knowing
- Curious
- Optimistic
- Slightly poetic
- Gently irreverent
- Direct when facts matter
- Never defensive

- Short declarative statements

- “World-class”
- “Unparalleled”

- “Indulge”
- “Oasis”
- “Hidden gem”
- “Something for everyone”
- Overstated wellness language
- Generic boutique-hotel copy

## Expectation-Setting Tone

Be positive, specific, and plainspoken.

Preferred:

> Your cabin is private, while the environment around it is shared. Open ceilings allow air to move throughout the floor, and they also mean sound and light can travel between cabins.

Avoid:

> Enjoy our unique semi-open concept.

The first communicates the product. The second hides it.

# 15. Accessibility and Usability

The visual identity can be expressive without sacrificing access.

## Requirements

- Maintain sufficient color contrast.
- Never place essential information only inside images.
- Provide useful alt text.
- Respect reduced-motion preferences.
- Ensure marquees and animations can pause.
- Keep booking CTAs keyboard accessible.
- Preserve visible focus states.
- Use semantic heading order.
- Make room facts available without hover.
- Avoid tiny condensed body text.
- Avoid text over complex imagery without a stable contrast layer.
- Ensure all guest restrictions and accessibility facts are readable on mobile.
- Do not hide key disclosures in accordions by default when they affect purchase suitability.

# 16. Responsive Behavior

## Mobile Priority

The majority of travel discovery and booking interactions may occur on mobile, so the mobile experience must preserve:

- Brand impact
- Product clarity
- Fast booking access
- Room-type distinctions
- Expectation setting
- Image quality
- Readable editorial content

## Mobile Rules

- Keep hero headlines short.
- Avoid overlapping type that becomes illegible.
- Stack split-screen modules intentionally.
- Keep room facts above the booking CTA.
- Preserve “two separate cabins” language for Duo products.
- Avoid autoplay-heavy experiences.
- Compress imagery without flattening color.
- Ensure sticky booking actions do not cover content.
- Keep policy and rate terms visible before purchase.

- Does the page retain contemporary usability?
- Is the guest, not the hotel, positioned as protagonist?

## Product

- Is “private sleeper cabin” immediately clear?
- Are shared bathrooms explicit?
- Are open ceilings explicit?
- Is sound and light transfer explained?
- Is no elevator disclosed?
- Is centrally managed temperature disclosed?
- Are quiet rules clear?
- Is luggage guidance clear?
- Are Duo products clearly two separate cabins?

## Visual

- Is one feature dominant?
- Are colors used in large, confident fields?
- Is condensed type reserved for display?
- Is body copy readable?
- Do imagery and graphics feel editorial rather than corporate?
- Are gradients analog and dreamlike rather than tech-like?

## Content
